I Replaced 5 Paid Apps with Open Source — Here's My Exact Stack

Hook (0:00–0:30)
I was bleeding $147 every single month on software subscriptions. Notion, 1Password, Figma, Todoist, Lightroom — all solid tools, but my credit card was screaming. Then I went nuclear. I wiped the slate clean and rebuilt my entire productivity stack using only open-source software. Same output. Zero licensing fees. And honestly? Some of these free tools run circles around the paid versions I left behind. Here is the exact five-app swap that saved me nearly two grand a year.

Content

Section 1: Notion → Logseq: The Second Brain That Actually Belongs to You
I used to treat Notion like my external brain. Every article draft, project timeline, and random shower thought lived inside those glossy little blocks. Until the day I was on a four-hour flight with no Wi-Fi and needed to access my content calendar. I opened Notion. I stared at a spinning wheel for twenty minutes. That was the moment I realized I wasn't building a second brain — I was renting storage in someone else's warehouse, and the landlord had just changed the locks. Switching to Logseq felt like moving from a furnished apartment into a house I actually owned. It's an open-source, local-first knowledge graph that stores everything in plain Markdown files on your actual hard drive. I recreated my entire content pipeline using daily journals, linked references, and simple page properties. Now, when I tag a video idea with 'script-pending,' it automatically surfaces in my weekly review query. The graph view even showed me connections between topics I didn't realize were related — something Notion's rigid hierarchy never revealed. I sync it to my private Git repository for backup, which costs me nothing. Best part? During another flight — same laptop, zero internet — I pulled up Logseq and kept working seamlessly. My thoughts, my files, my machine. That's the difference between borrowing and owning your ideas.

Section 2: 1Password → Bitwarden: Your Keys, Your Kingdom
Password managers are supposed to be vaults, but most people are storing their gold in a safety deposit box where the bank controls the key. When LastPass had its catastrophic breach, I looked at my 1Password subscription and felt a familiar itch: what if the next headline was theirs? Exporting my data felt intentionally clunky, like a gym making cancellation harder than signup. That's not security; that's custody. Bitwarden is fully open source, which means its code is audited by thousands of eyes worldwide. I migrated three hundred passwords, credit cards, and secure notes in under an hour using a simple CSV import. The mobile app syncs instantly and the biometric unlock is smoother than 1Password's was on my phone. For most users, the free tier is genuinely enough — unlimited passwords across unlimited devices. But I went a step further and spun up Vaultwarden, a lightweight self-hosted fork, on a cheap home server. Now my encrypted vault lives on hardware I control. The browser extension auto-fills faster than 1Password ever did, and the built-in TOTP generator means I don't need a separate authenticator app. It's like building your own panic room: you still get bank-grade encryption, but you hold the only key.

Section 3: Figma → Penpot: Design Without the Paywall
Figma is the industry standard for a reason, but industry standards love to move the goalposts. When they locked developer handoff and advanced prototyping behind a higher paywall last year, my monthly bill for two designer seats jumped overnight. It reminded me of a carpenter who builds an entire workshop inside someone else's garage, then wakes up to find the rent doubled and the power tools chained up. Penpot is the open-source design platform that runs entirely in your browser, just like Figma, but with zero strings attached. Because it's built on SVG standards, every shape, board, and component you create is native web code — not a proprietary blob. I rebuilt my entire YouTube thumbnail system inside Penpot in a weekend. The flex layout system actually handles responsive design better than Figma's constraints, and sharing a prototype with a freelancer is as simple as sending a URL. No seat limits. No dev mode upsell. If Penpot ever disappears, I still own every SVG file. Try saying that about a Figma design locked in the cloud. The learning curve is about forty-eight hours; the freedom is permanent.

Section 4: Todoist → Super Productivity: Tasks on Steroids
Todoist is beautiful, but it's also a masterclass in the productivity tax — charging you subscription fees for features that should be table stakes. I remember missing a client deadline because Todoist hid the reminder behind a premium badge. Imagine buying a car and discovering the brakes are part of a DLC pack. That's when I started hunting for a task manager that respects both my time and my wallet. Super Productivity is an open-source desktop app that looks simple until you realize it's a tactical command center. The keyboard shortcuts alone saved me more time in a week than Todoist did in a month. I used it to produce this very video. I broke the script into subtasks, assigned time estimates, and used the built-in Pomodoro timer to stay honest during writing sessions. When I dragged a task onto the calendar view, it automatically blocked out research time and flagged conflicts with my recording schedule. It even tracks how long I actually spend versus what I predicted, so my estimates get sharper every week. There are no artificial caps on projects, no paywalls on notifications, and no cloud required. It lives on your machine, starts in under a second, and treats you like a power user from day one.

Section 5: Adobe Lightroom → Darktable: Raw Power, Zero Rent
Adobe's photography ecosystem is a velvet trap. They hook you with student pricing, then slowly crank the subscription dial while your entire photo library gets glued to their cloud. Last year, my Creative Cloud bill crossed two hundred dollars annually just for Lightroom and Photoshop. I had fifty thousand RAW photos — essentially my visual memory — locked inside an ecosystem that could change pricing, terms, or access whenever they wanted. It's like developing photos in a darkroom where the landlord can walk in and turn off the safelights. Darktable is the open-source RAW processor that professional photographers secretly swear by. I imported my entire 4TB Sony A7IV image archive in one evening. The filmic RGB module gives me color grading control that rivals Lightroom's develop panel, and every single edit is saved as a sidecar file next to the original RAW. That means if I uninstall Darktable tomorrow, my edits are still readable, portable, and mine. I built a custom preset stack for my YouTube thumbnails so every photo starts with the same baseline contrast and skin tone protection. No monthly fee. No cloud upload bottleneck. Just raw processing power that respects the fact that your portfolio is a lifetime asset, not a rental.
