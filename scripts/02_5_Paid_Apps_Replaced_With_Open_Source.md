# I Replaced 5 Paid Apps With Open Source

## Hook
Last month, I canceled five software subscriptions that were costing me two hundred and forty dollars a month. Together. That is nearly three grand a year going to tools I thought I needed. Here is the part that actually hurt: four of them had free replacements that were better. Not cheaper. Not good enough. Actually better. I am going to show you the exact replacements, why they work, and the one place where paying still makes sense. Because some subscription companies are charging you for convenience you already own.

## Content

### The Adobe Tax Is Over
I used to pay sixty dollars a month for Adobe Creative Cloud. Photoshop, Illustrator, Premiere. The whole suite. I told myself I needed it for work. Then I found GIMP. Free, open-source, and for the editing I actually do, it handles everything. Layer masks, color grading, batch processing. Then I replaced Illustrator with Inkscape. Vector editing, logo design, SVG exports. Free. Open source. Runs faster. The learning curve exists, but it is a weekend of tutorials, not a semester. The industry has convinced us that creative work requires Adobe. It does not. It requires skill.

### The Notion Premium Trap
Notion is great. I used it for years. Then they started adding AI features behind paywalls. I had hundreds of pages and synced databases. But here is what I realized: Obsidian is local-first, your data lives on your machine, and the core app is completely free. Plugins handle everything Notion charges for. Graph view, daily notes, spaced repetition. Two thousand free plugins in the community. I moved my entire knowledge base in three hours. The search is faster. The backlinks are smarter. And nobody changes the pricing next quarter. Your second brain should not be rented.

### When Microsoft Office Becomes Unnecessary
I paid for Office because that is what you do. Word, Excel, PowerPoint. One hundred fifty dollars a year now. LibreOffice replaced all of it. Writer handles contracts. Calc handles spreadsheets. Impress handles presentations. File compatibility is ninety-five percent, and for everything that matters, it just works. No cloud dependency. No OneDrive syncing issues. No sudden feature moves. Files live on your machine. Formatting stays stable. If you are not sending government contracts all day, LibreOffice is all the office you need.

### Replacing Slack With Something Better
Slack costs eight dollars per user monthly. For five people, that is forty dollars monthly. Four hundred eighty yearly. For a chat app. Element, built on Matrix, is free. End-to-end encrypted. Federated. Voice calls, screen sharing, file uploads. The interface is clean. The mobile app is solid. Your conversations are not mined for enterprise data. I switched my team six months ago. Nobody asked to go back. Onboarding takes ten minutes per person instead of two. That is a half hour one-time cost versus forty dollars monthly forever. Do the math.

### The One Place Where Paying Still Matters
Here is the honest truth. Not everything should be free. I still pay for ProtonMail because email security matters and running your own mail server is a nightmare. I still pay for a domain registrar and basic hosting. The rule is this: pay for infrastructure you cannot easily replace and security that actually protects you. But for creative tools, productivity suites, and communication platforms, the open-source alternatives have caught up. In some cases they have surpassed the paid options. The only thing stopping people is the friction of switching. And this video just removed that excuse.
